02 — Gallery

— Pastel Case Study

A simple collaborative communication platform.

This is a self-directed product design case study. The majority of this case study was produced over a two-week period. The output was created using Sketch, Illustrator and Origami.

Problem

There is no way to easily collaborate on creative projects outside of the workplace.

Solution

Leverage conversation threading to create a simple and organized productivity platform.

Overview

Research - Introduction

I have found that creatives are being underserved by the collaboration products available to them online. Most productivity tools seem to cater to projects that happen within a workplace or in large static teams.

Independent creatives however such as photographers, videographers, musicians, fashion stylists, visual artists and designers are likely to work fluidly with multiple different groups of collaborators, on a multitude of different projects. This segment of workers seems to be overlooked.

It is also highly important to start to reconsider how we commonly define "work". Firstly, the classic idea of the workplace is dissolving and being replaced by platforms that treat workers as interchangeable contractors. Soon, most work will be categorized as freelance. Secondly, when automation and universal basic income is discussed, it is always followed by the question: "What will people do with their time?". A typical response to this question is that people will fill their need for work by pursuing creative endeavours. What would a product look like that responds to this need and provides a platform for independent, creative work?

Research - Competitive Analysis

Are any products already solving this problem?

As I began to consider what platforms could already fill this need, I developed four qualifiers. These are qualities that are essential for the product to possess in order to solve the problem I've highlighted above.



Mobile

Is the platform mobile-first? While mobile is far surpassing desktop in usage overall, it is especially necessary that the product be mobile-oriented as creative freelancers are going to be less attached to a physical work location.

Organized

Is the platform organized? Being able to separate content and keep projects organized is essential while collaborating.

Casual

Can the platform be used casually? This is the most nuanced quality, but one that I believe is the most necessary. Some platforms are built for organizations with established teams and long term projects and are therefore very rigid. The platform needs to be able to respond fluidly to the dynamic nature of creative work.

Instant

Is communication instant? The platform needs to be capable of instant messaging, as collaboration requires extensive and immediate communication.



None of the most obvious platforms fill the role defined by these qualifiers:

Slack

While Slack and other similar workplace tools are organized, instant, and available on mobile, they are structured in a way that is only useful for established teams. Workspaces and threads on Slack are built for teams that consistently work together and have established projects and topics. If someone wanted to collaborate separately with one other individual that wasn't a part of this team, they would need to create an entirely new workspace. Even then, the threading system within that workspace is structured to facilitate ongoing communication around one topic. Fluidity is a core principle the feature needs to exhibit.

Text Message (iMessage/SMS/Messenger)

The creatives I spoke with primarily used messaging apps for communicating during projects. While one can appreciate the ease of communication that these products provide, there are no ways of organizing content. Scrolling up in a thread to try to find an important link or message is a common and frustrating experience for many users, myself included.

Email

Email is highly organized but its format is simply too slow and not instant.

Other

There are a few other productivity applications that are worth quickly mentioning. Google Drive and Dropbox are primarily storage products with some secondary commenting features. Pinterest, Instagram, and Are.na are all great ways to collect visual inspiration but aren’t helpful general purpose tools.



Each of these platforms possess primitives that could be reorganized into a new format. The threading in Slack and email is useful, while the simplicity and speed of messaging apps is ideal. What would a product look like that synthesizes these elements?

Research - Personas

I spoke with some creative friends and developed a few personas and use cases to try to further understand who would benefit from using this product.

Claire is a touring music producer and DJ who collaborates with a range of other creatives and professionals. Claire is in constant contact with her management, designers, and other musicians about dates, legal agreements, work-in-progress music, strategy, artwork, social media content and more. For eight months of the year she is traveling.

Julian is a freelance fashion stylist. He has been in the industry for five years. He is constantly finding new clients, and has a large set of co-collaborators. As a freelance stylist, half of his time is spent with a core group of clients, and the other half is spent on one-time video shoots and shows. He has to coordinate the sourcing of clothes, schedule and organize shoots, search for talent, collaborate on style direction and more. He always wants to expand his network of collaborators and connections.

Sandeep is a photographer, videographer and content producer. He just graduated college and is establishing himself in the field. He uses a computer when he edits, but spends most of his time shooting. As video productions requires a lot of specialized roles, he is always finding new directors, cinematographers, editors, designers, VFX artists, writers and actors to work with.

Process - Site Map

After identifying the foundational elements of the app, including chat and conversation threading, I started to structure the main pages.

Process - Wireframes

I created and iterated on a rough set of wireframes for the main screens of the app and started to flesh out the details of the features that will be on each page.

Using the first set of wireframes, I quickly created a rough low-fidelity prototype to start to validate the concept. I gained some insightful feedback from this prototype, such as the need for version control. In the next section I will go over the final features.

First prototype

Features

Home screen

Pastel opens to a standard list. Here, users can see a set of conversations with people they have added as contacts. This functions similarly to a regular messaging app, as each conversation is entirely separate from one another. The user can also create a new conversation with a group of collaborators.

Start State

The Board

When a user clicks on a conversation, they are shown a board. A board is a private space between the participants of that conversation and includes a list of items that the participants have added. These could include content such as links, files, photos or videos. A separate chat is appended to each item of the board. This way users can quickly discuss topics or portions of projects in an organized way, but not get too stuck in a specific structure. When someone writes in a chat, or a new item is added, that item is pushed to the top of the list into the "recent" section.

Board

Adding Elements to the Board

The user can either upload content or link to an external file to add a new item to the board. New items or ones with recent chat activity get bumped to the top of the list.

Adding Content

Chat

A new chat is created for each item on the board. This way, multiple items can be discussed simultaneously without getting mixed up. When there are new messages in a chat, the chat bubble on the element appears darker and displays the number of messages.

Chat

Viewing content and Version Control

Clicking on the item displays its content. Each piece of content has a very simple form of version control. If content is updated, a small label (see above) will appear in the chat to notify other users. The user can revert, update, or add pieces of content to the item.

Version Control

Groups

Multiple collaborators can be added to the board to turn it into a group.

Groups

Brand

I began to envision how this product would be branded and marketed. As the general public is waking up to the reality of technology addiction, I believe there is a desire for platforms that enable productivity instead of disrupting it. People aspire to be productive and creative but don't necessarily have the ability to do so.

Platforms such as YouTube and Instagram have helped give rise to a highly visible creative culture. To be able to work on creative projects is seen as a goal to strive for. There is an opportunity in this product to create a lifestyle software brand that is actually aspirational and focused on growth, creativity, and productivity.

In contrast to a lot of complex professional collaboration tools, I wanted the visual design of the platform to reflect this idea of simple creative communication.

Complete prototype

Here is the final prototype and design for Pastel. Together, these features could allow creatives to quickly switch between collaborators, projects, and ideas and work on them in a fluid but organized way. This would address a lot of pain points that creatives currently have when collaborating, as it fulfills all four qualifiers I established earlier.

Closing

Overall, this was an attempt to design a product that I believe could fill a gap in the market. During the course of this project, I learned the benefits of developing parameters early. By defining the problem by a set of qualifiers, it enabled me to better understand what the successful product would look like. If I could progress further with this project, I would focus on developing this beyond an MVP and establish a more robust set of features.

I had a lot of fun creating this case study.
